When dealing with a corpus containing thousands of unique tokens, a standard bar chart of every token becomes unreadable due to the "long tail" of infrequent words.

In Natural Language Processing, there are two primary ways to visualize this data effectively, depending on whether you want to see specific words or the overall distribution of the language.

1. The Frequency Distribution Plot (Top N Tokens)
If your goal is to identify the most common words (often used to find "stop words" like the, is, at), you should use a Sorted Bar Chart or a Line Plot limited to the top 30–50 tokens.

X-axis: Tokens (ranked from most to least frequent).

Y-axis: Absolute frequency (count) or relative frequency (percentage).

Why it works: It provides immediate insight into the most dominant terms in your specific dataset.

2. The Log-Log Plot (Zipf’s Law)
If you want to visualize the entire corpus (all thousands of tokens), a standard linear scale won't work because a few words appear millions of times while most appear only once or twice. Instead, you should use a Log-Log Plot.

Natural language typically follows Zipf’s Law, which states that the frequency of a word is inversely proportional to its rank. When you plot log(rank) against log(frequency), the result should be a roughly downward-sloping straight line.

X-axis: log(Rank) (where the most frequent word is rank 1).

Y-axis: log(Frequency).

Why it works: It compresses the massive differences in scale, allowing you to see the "Head" (common words), the "Torso," and the "Long Tail" (rare words or hapax legomena) all in one view.

3. Cumulative Frequency Plot
Another useful visualization is a Cumulative Distribution Plot. This helps you understand "vocabulary coverage."

X-axis: Number of unique tokens (sorted by rank).

Y-axis: Cumulative percentage of the total words in the corpus.

Insight: You will often find that a very small percentage of unique tokens (e.g., the top 100 words) accounts for 50% or more of the entire volume of text in your corpus.

Summary Recommendation
To see which words are common: Bar Chart of the Top 50 tokens.

To see the mathematical behavior of your corpus: Log-Log Zipf Plot.

To decide how many words to keep in your vocabulary: Cumulative Frequency Plot.

DF vs IDF

It is common to get these two confused because they are two sides of the same coin. The simplest way to think about them is: Document Frequency (DF) is the raw count of "popularity," while Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) is the mathematical "weight" or "importance" of a word.

Here is a breakdown of the differences:

1. Document Frequency (DF)
Definition: The number of documents in your corpus that contain a specific term.

Scale: Linear (e.g., if a word is in 10 documents, DF=10).

What it tells us: How "common" a word is across the entire dataset.

The Problem: High DF words (like "the," "is," "of") are usually the least informative words in a document because they appear everywhere.

2. Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)
Definition: A logarithmic calculation that "flips" the DF value. It is the total number of documents divided by the DF.

Scale: Logarithmic.

What it tells us: How "informative" or "unique" a word is.

The Solution: IDF rewards rare words with a high score and penalizes common words with a low score (often approaching 0).

How do i determine IDF, and TF thresholds for stop-word detection?

Determining thresholds for stop-word detection is a balancing act. If your thresholds are too aggressive, you lose context (e.g., "Not good" becomes just "good"). If they are too relaxed, your model gets bogged down by noise.

In NLP, we typically use a combination of statistical analysis and corpus-specific insights to set these "cutoff" points.

1. Determining the IDF Threshold
Since IDF measures how unique a word is across the corpus, a low IDF score indicates a stop-word.

The Logarithmic Logic: Because IDF uses a log scale, the values are usually small (e.g., between 0 and 15).

The "Zero" Rule: Any word with an IDF=0 (or very close to it) appears in every single document. These are 100% stop-words for your specific dataset.

The Elbow Method: Plot your terms sorted by IDF. You will see a sharp "elbow" where the values start to climb. Words below this elbow are your "corpus-specific stop-words" (e.g., in a medical dataset, the word "patient" might have a very low IDF and act like a stop-word).

2. Determining the TF Threshold (Max DF)
In libraries like Scikit-Learn, this is often handled by a parameter called max_df.

Percentage-based Threshold: A common starting point is 0.70 to 0.90. This means "ignore any word that appears in more than 90% of the documents."

Absolute-count Threshold: If your corpus is small, you might set an absolute limit (e.g., "ignore words appearing in more than 500 documents").

The Logic: High Document Frequency (DF) is the most reliable indicator of a functional stop-word. If "the" appears in 99% of your training data, it provides zero predictive power for classification.

3. The "Min DF" Threshold (The Tail)
While not strictly for "stop-words," setting a minimum threshold is equally important for cleaning.

Rare Word Filtering: Words that appear in only 1 or 2 documents (out of thousands) are often typos, rare names, or noise.

Standard Practice: Setting min_df=2 or min_df=5 significantly reduces the dimensionality of your feature matrix without losing meaningful signal.

Explain MLE

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is the most fundamental approach to estimating the parameters of a statistical model. In the context of Natural Language Processing (NLP) and n-gram language models, its goal is simple: find the probability distribution that makes the observed training data most likely to have occurred.

1. The Core Philosophy
Imagine you have a bag of marbles (some red, some blue), and you want to guess the true ratio of red to blue marbles. You pull out 10 marbles and find that 7 are red and 3 are blue.

MLE says: "Based on my observation, the most likely true ratio in the bag is exactly 70% red and 30% blue." It assumes the data you have collected is the "best" representation of reality.

MLE is easy to compute and very intuitive, but it has one massive, fatal flaw in language modeling: The Zero Probability Problem.

The Problem: If you have a sequence that never appeared in your training corpus (e.g., "artificial cat"), the Count(w1, w2) will be 0. Consequently, the probability P becomes 0.

The Consequence: Because language models calculate sentence probability by multiplying word probabilities together, if a single word pair has a probability of 0, the entire sentence probability becomes 0. This happens even if the sentence is grammatically and semantically perfect.

Here is the standard workflow for an MLE n-gram model:

1. The Pre-calculation Phase (Training)
Before you ever ask the model to predict a word, you must process your entire training corpus. During this stage, you do not calculate probabilities; instead, you build a Frequency Lookup Table (often a nested dictionary or a hash map).

You store: Count(w1) and Count(w1, w2).

explain the main principle of llr

The main principle of the Log-Likelihood Ratio (LLR) in the context of collocation discovery is Hypothesis Testing. It measures how much more likely the observed data is under the assumption that two words are dependent on each other, compared to the assumption that they are completely independent.

Unlike the t-test, which compares means, LLR compares the entire distribution of a word across two different contexts.

1. The "Two Worlds" Comparison

If the ratio is massive, the Dependence World is a much better explanation (strong collocation).

Computation: It turns tiny, complex decimal multiplications into simple additions.

Distribution: The resulting value (multiplied by -2) follows a Chi-square distribution, making it easy to use standard statistical thresholds (like 3.84 or 10.83) to determine significance.

4. Why it is the "Gold Standard"
The main principle that makes LLR superior to the t-test or Chi-square for language is its sensitivity to rare events.

The t-test assumes that word frequencies follow a "Normal Distribution" (the bell curve). However, language is "Zipfian"—most words are extremely rare. The t-test often fails to handle these rare words correctly.

LLR is a "likelihood" test based on binomial distributions. It doesn't care if the data is rare or frequent; it only cares about the evidence of a shift in probability. This is why it works perfectly for both high-frequency pairs like "of the" and rare technical terms that only appear 5 times.
