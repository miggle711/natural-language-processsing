DU2 Stopword Identification(5 marks)
A stopword is a token that appears so frequently across the corpus that it carries little
meaningful information. To identify stopwords from the training data, you can use two
measures:
Term Frequency (TF)is the proportion of times a token appears out of all tokens
in the corpus. For example, if the corpus has 100,000 tokens and a token appears 500
times, its TF is 500/100,000 = 0.5%.
Document Frequency (DF)is the proportion of documents in which a token ap-
pears at least once. For example, if there are 8,000 documents and a token appears in
2,000 of them, its DF is 2,000/8,000 = 25%.
Note:In practice, stopword detection is often based onInverse Document Frequency
(IDF). A low IDF indicates that a token appears in many documents and is therefore less
informative. We use DF directly here for simplicity and intuition, but the two approaches
are equivalent, a high DF corresponds to a low IDF. Refer to this resource for a more
detailed explanation of TF-IDF.
Note that even without access to the actual words, the statistical characteristics of stop-
words remain consistent. Tokens that are stopwords will exhibit the same distributional
patterns regardless of whether you can decode them. This means you can identify stop-
words purely from the token indices and their frequency statistics in the training data.
Report a list of 40 stopword token IDs that you identified. Identifying stopwords is
beneficial for a later task in this assignment. Compute both measures using the training
data only.

A2 Catchphrase Insertion(20 marks)
Large language models are now used by virtually everyone to generate text, raising a
natural question: how do you leave your mark on what you create? In this task you will
explore a small-scale version of text watermarking by embedding a secretcatchphrase
into text generated by your n-gram model. Chooseone category( Type 1 or Type 2,..
etc) from your dataset and perform all insertion and evaluation on documents from that
category only. You may choose the n-gram order and model of your choice for this
task, with justification. Observations must be clearly reported in your notebook with
supporting statistics. You will demonstrate the this using Variations 1 and 2 below.
Catchphrase selection(2 marks)
Choose a catchphrase of three or more tokens that containsno stopwordsand whose
tokens arestatistically rarein the training corpus. A trivially common phrase would not
constitute a convincing watermark. For example, a phrase whose constituent bigrams each
account for less than 30% of all bigrams starting with that context, i.e.P(w 2 |w 1)<0.30
andP(w 3 |w 2)<0.20, could be considered rare. You may also use alternative statistical
evidence such as low unigram frequency, high IDF, etc, to justify your selection. Since
you are working with token indices rather than words, you will not be penalised for
the semantic quality of the phrase, what matters is that you can demonstrate its rarity
statistically using token counts.
Variation 1 — Data-level insertion(6 marks)
Modify thetraining dataso that the catchphrase becomes more probable under the
learned n-gram model, for example by injecting the catchphrase into training documents
to increase its observed n-gram counts. In no more than two sentences, explain how you
modified the data and why this raises the catchphrase probability.
Variation 2 — Model-level insertion(6 marks)
Leave the training data unchanged and instead modify themodel or its sampling
procedure, for example by biasing the next-token distribution toward catchphrase tokens
or using forced insertion at fixed intervals. In no more than two sentences, explain what
you changed and how it encourages catchphrase appearance.
